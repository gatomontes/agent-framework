# Blackquill's Audit: Web Agency Growth Engine Skill

**Audited:** 2026-04-09  
**Target:** `web-agency-growth-skill.md` (WA-GROWTH-01)

---

## Executive Summary

This skill is a **competently assembled delusion** — a framework that looks like a system but will collapse the moment a real client with a real budget and real constraints sits at the table. It confuses *procedure* with *progress*.

---

## The Core Problem: Process Theater

This skill lists 6 stages with sub-bullets and deliverables as if the act of documentation creates competence. It doesn't. A bad team following this perfectly will still produce garbage. A good team doesn't need 80% of this scaffolding.

**What's missing:** A mechanism to identify *capability gaps before they become failures*. You cannot "orchestrate" personas that don't exist. If your "BI Analyst" is a junior with Google Analytics access and no statistical training, your "LTV:CAC calculations" are fictional.

---

## Greenfield Path: The Fantasy of Benchmark Data

> "Calculate industry benchmark conversion rates... Define target LTV:CAC ratio (minimum 3:1)."

**Who is calculating this?** A junior pulling numbers from a blog post? A senior analyst who understands cohort leakage? The skill assumes the *role* produces the *competence*. It doesn't.

Also: "5-7 user interviews" — in what universe does a greenfield project have budget for user research before the client has signed off on wireframes? This is academic. Real agencies sell wireframes first, then discover the client's actual market is nothing like the research suggested.

---

## Brownfield Path: The Pretending-to-Data Problem

> "Pull 6-12 months of conversion data... Run cohort analysis... Reconcile analytics against payment processor data."

**Lovely in theory. In practice:**

- Most small/medium clients have broken analytics. Event tracking is incomplete. Attribution is a joke.
- "Reconciliation" requires access to payment processor accounts — which clients frequently refuse to provide.
- Cohort analysis on 6 months of data from a site with 500 transactions/month is statistical noise dressed as insight.

The skill assumes *clean data exists*. It rarely does. Where's the "data quality triage" step? Where's the "your analytics is broken, here's what we can salvage" protocol?

---

## The Persona Shell Game

| Persona | Reality Check |
|---------|---------------|
| BI Analyst | Most agencies don't have one. They have a "marketing person who looks at GA4 sometimes." |
| CRO Strategist | Rare. Usually a web designer who read a CXL blog post. |
| Direct-Response Copywriter | The rarest. Most agencies use the client's copy or SEO content writers. |
| Growth Engineer | If you have one, they're building the site, not "instrumenting events." |
| Project Manager | Exists, but is usually managing 12 other projects. |

The skill orchestrates *roles*, not *humans*. If the agency is a solo freelancer, they're expected to wear all five hats. That's not orchestration — that's burnout disguised as methodology.

---

## The Statistical Illiteracy Problem

> "Run an A/A test to confirm <5% false positive rate."
> "Calculate required sample size for statistical significance (95% confidence, 80% power)."

**This is cargo-cult statistics.** An A/A test doesn't "confirm" your tool works — it tells you if your implementation is broken. A 5% false positive rate is the *expected* noise floor, not a benchmark to beat.

And sample size calculations assume *consistent traffic*. A seasonal business (most ecom clients) needs to account for temporal variance. A B2B lead gen site with 50 conversions/month will never reach statistical significance in any reasonable timeframe.

Where's the Bayesian alternative? Where's the "we don't have enough data, here's how to make decisions anyway" fallback?

---

## What's Actually Good

The **hypothesis format** is solid:
> "If we [change], then [metric] will [improve] because [reason]."

That's falsifiable. That's good. The rest of the skill should be built around *that discipline*, not around stage gates that create the illusion of control.

The **leakage quantification** concept is valuable. Framing fixes in revenue terms ("Fixing this would increase revenue by 33%") is how you sell CRO. More of this, less of the 6-stage theater.

---

## The Missing Pieces

1. **Client Qualification Protocol:** How do you know this framework applies to *this* client? A $5K budget can't afford Stage 0. A $500K budget doesn't need this much structure.
2. **Data Triage:** What do you do when the data is garbage? (Answer: most of the time.)
3. **Capability Assessment:** Before orchestrating personas, assess if they exist. If they don't, what's the degraded mode?
4. **Decision Velocity:** The skill assumes perfect information. Real decisions are made with incomplete data. Where's the "good enough" threshold?
5. **Kill Criteria:** When do you tell the client "this isn't working" and stop taking their money?

---

## Summary

This skill is a **consultant's fantasy** — what agencies *wish* their process looked like. It will fail in implementation because it assumes competence where none exists, data where there is noise, and resources where there are constraints.

**Fix it or it will fix you:** Start with capability assessment, add data triage, define degraded modes, and for god's sake, include a "fire the client" protocol.
