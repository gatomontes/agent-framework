# Citadel Audit Protocol

## Purpose

The Audit Protocol defines how The Citadel evaluates the integrity of verification, critique, evidence handling, escalation behavior, and operational trust claims.

Audit exists to prevent performative verification.

---

# Core Principle

Verification itself must remain verifiable.

---

# Audit Goals

Audit should determine:

- whether verification was genuine
- whether evidence lineage remained intact
- whether critique was substantive
- whether escalation was suppressed
- whether confidence claims were justified
- whether operational law was preserved
- whether restoration was valid

Audit evaluates process integrity, not merely artifact existence.

---

# Audit Triggers

Audit should activate when:

- consequence tier is critical
- verification confidence is disputed
- contradictory evidence exists
- restoration occurred
- escalation chains became complex
- operational trust is strategically important
- runtime instability appeared
- governance drift is suspected

---

# Audit Evidence

Audits should preserve:

- operational states
- verification artifacts
- critique outputs
- escalation history
- restoration history
- runtime logs
- authority chain
- artifact lineage

An audit without reconstructable evidence is weak audit.

---

# Audit Findings

Audit may conclude:

- VERIFIED_PROCESS
- PROCESS_FAILURE
- INSUFFICIENT_EVIDENCE
- GOVERNANCE_BREACH
- RESTORATION_INVALID
- UNCERTAIN

Audit findings should preserve explanatory reasoning.

---

# Audit Independence

Critical audits should favor independence from:

- original executors
- original verifiers
- original runtime pathways

Systems that audit themselves without separation accumulate epistemic drift.

---

# Audit and Trust

Audit does not manufacture trust.

Audit evaluates whether trust claims were operationally justified.

---

# Constitutional Rule

A system that cannot audit its own verification cannot sustain trustworthy governance.
