# Verification Contract

## Status

Canonical Citadel contract.

This document defines how Citadel evaluates whether operational outputs, claims, actions, evidence, and knowledge satisfy institutional acceptance requirements.

Verification is the bridge between:
- execution
- epistemology
- authority
- institutional trust

Without verification:
- evidence becomes performance
- execution becomes theater
- governance becomes ceremonial
- autonomy becomes unverifiable

---

# Purpose

The Verification Contract exists to prevent:
- false completion
- confidence laundering
- unverifiable claims
- hidden contradictions
- audit collapse
- authority self-certification
- synthetic certainty
- institutional drift

Verification protects institutional coherence.

---

# Core Principle

Execution is not verification.

Confidence is not verification.

Consensus is not verification.

Verification requires:
- evidence
- provenance
- reproducibility
- coherence
- uncertainty preservation
- auditability

---

# Verification States

All verification outcomes must remain classifiable.

Minimum verification states:
- unverified
- partially-verified
- verified
- conditionally-verified
- contradicted
- unverifiable
- stale
- superseded

Verification state may change as evidence evolves.

# Verification Inputs

Verification may evaluate:
- artifacts
- claims
- operational actions
- mission outcomes
- runtime behavior
- evidence chains
- topology integrity
- authority compliance
- restoration outcomes
- epistemic integrity

Verification scope must remain explicit.

# Verification Requirements

Verification should preserve:
- evidence provenance
- contradiction visibility
- uncertainty state
- authority boundaries
- transformation visibility
- audit traceability
- reproducibility when possible

Verification must not erase complexity for convenience.

---

# Verification Evidence Classes

Verification may rely upon:

## Direct Evidence

Evidence directly observing the claimed condition.

Examples:
- command output
- raw logs
- screenshots
- test execution
- source files
- measurements

## Derived Evidence

Evidence inferred through reasoning from direct evidence.

Inference strength must remain explicit.

## Corroborative Evidence

Independent evidence supporting a claim.

## Negative Evidence

Evidence contradicting or weakening a claim.

Negative evidence must remain visible.

## Synthetic Evidence

Generated evidence or model-produced interpretation.

Synthetic evidence requires elevated scrutiny.

---

# Verification Separation

Verification authority should remain separate from execution authority whenever practical.

Critique should remain structurally distinct from both execution and verification whenever the mission consequence or uncertainty profile justifies it.

Default institutional rule:

```txt
The producer of a claim is not the final verifier of that claim.
```

Self-verification requires:
- explicit authority
- elevated evidence standards
- preserved audit visibility
- contradiction tolerance

---

# Verification Depth

Verification depth should scale with:
- mission risk
- reversibility
- authority sensitivity
- external impact
- operational complexity
- epistemic uncertainty
- institutional dependency load

Not all missions require maximal verification.

All missions require explicit verification posture.

---

# Verification Failure

Verification failure occurs when:
- evidence is insufficient
- contradictions remain unresolved
- provenance is missing
- reproducibility fails
- authority boundaries were violated
- uncertainty was concealed
- auditability collapses
- stale assumptions invalidate conclusions

Verification failure does not necessarily imply maliciousness.

It indicates institutional insufficiency.

---

# Contradiction Handling

Verification must preserve contradiction visibility.

Contradictions may result in:
- restoration
- escalation
- replayed verification
- topology reassessment
- authority review
- mission suspension
- conditional acceptance

Verification may not suppress contradictions to preserve narrative coherence.

---

# Uncertainty Preservation

Verification must preserve unresolved uncertainty.

Verification may conclude:
- evidence supports the claim
- evidence weakens the claim
- evidence remains insufficient
- contradictions remain unresolved

Verification must not fabricate certainty to satisfy operational momentum.

---

# Reproducibility

When possible, verification should preserve reproducibility.

Reproducibility may include:
- replayable commands
- reproducible tests
- artifact reconstruction
- traceable transformations
- deterministic verification routes

Non-reproducible verification must explicitly declare limitations.

---

# Freshness Semantics

Verification decays over time.

Verified status may become stale due to:
- environmental change
- dependency change
- topology change
- governance updates
- contradiction emergence
- operational drift
- evidence invalidation

Stale verification may require replay.

---

# Verification Drift

Verification drift occurs when institutional standards gradually weaken.

Signals include:
- accepting screenshots without traceability
- reduced evidence rigor
- suppression of uncertainty
- verification shortcuts normalized
- authority substituting for evidence
- ceremonial review behavior
- audit gaps ignored
- reproduction no longer attempted

Verification drift is governance corruption.

---

# Audit Requirements

Verification audit should reconstruct:
- what was verified
- what evidence was examined
- provenance chain
- contradiction history
- unresolved uncertainty
- verification methodology
- authority boundaries
- freshness state
- limitations
- final verification outcome

Verification must remain reconstructable.

---

# Restoration Interface

Verification may trigger restoration.

Restoration triggers include:
- unresolved contradiction
- invalidated evidence
- topology inconsistency
- authority violation
- stale verification
- audit collapse
- dependency invalidation

Verification restoration must remain auditable.

---

# Epistemic Interface

Verification does not create truth.

Verification evaluates institutional confidence relative to:
- evidence quality
- provenance quality
- contradiction pressure
- reproducibility
- uncertainty state

Verification must preserve epistemic integrity.

---

# Authority Interface

Authority may define:
- required verification depth
- acceptance thresholds
- escalation conditions
- verification scope

Authority may not redefine evidence quality arbitrarily.

Governance pressure does not convert weak evidence into verified truth.

---

# Runtime Independence

Verification belongs to Citadel doctrine.

It does not belong to:
- a runtime
- a model
- a persona
- a vendor
- a tooling ecosystem

Runtimes may participate in verification.

They do not own institutional truth semantics.

---

# Noncompliance

An actor violates verification doctrine if it:
- fabricates verification
- suppresses contradiction
- launders uncertainty into certainty
- weakens evidence requirements silently
- substitutes confidence for evidence
- hides provenance gaps
- performs ceremonial verification
- treats popularity as proof
- compresses audit visibility for convenience

Verification noncompliance requires review and possible restoration.
