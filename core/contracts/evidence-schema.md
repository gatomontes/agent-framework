# Evidence Schema

## Status

Canonical Citadel contract.

This document defines how Citadel structurally represents, weights, evaluates, propagates, preserves, and audits evidence.

The Evidence Schema exists to support:
- epistemic governance
- verification consistency
- contradiction handling
- provenance integrity
- confidence discipline
- institutional auditability

---

# Purpose

Citadel requires canonical evidence semantics because:
- evidence quality varies
- provenance quality varies
- confidence may become detached from support
- contradictions may be hidden
- verification may become ceremonial
- runtime outputs may appear authoritative without justification

Without evidence structure:
- epistemic doctrine becomes subjective
- verification becomes inconsistent
- trust propagation becomes unstable
- governance becomes vulnerable to confidence theater

---

# Core Principle

Evidence is not binary.

Evidence possesses:
- quality
- provenance
- freshness
- uncertainty
- contradiction exposure
- transformation history
- dependency impact

Institutional trust must remain proportional to evidence integrity.

# Evidence Types

## Direct

Direct observation of the claimed condition.

Examples:
- raw logs
- screenshots
- command outputs
- measurements
- source files
- firsthand observation

Direct evidence is not automatically complete.

---

## Derived

Generated through reasoning or transformation from other evidence.

Derived evidence must preserve:
- transformation visibility
- reasoning visibility
- dependency traceability

---

## Corroborative

Independent evidence supporting a claim.

Corroboration strength depends upon:
- source independence
- methodological independence
- provenance quality
- contradiction pressure

Repeated citation without independence is weak corroboration.

---

## Contradictory

Evidence weakening or opposing a claim.

Contradictory evidence must remain visible.

Contradictions are governance-relevant events.

---

## Synthetic

Generated by:
- models
- simulations
- transformations
- AI systems
- extrapolation systems

Synthetic evidence requires elevated scrutiny.

Synthetic evidence is not automatically invalid.

Synthetic evidence is not automatically trustworthy.

---

# Provenance States

Minimum provenance states:
- complete
- partial
- missing
- disputed
- reconstructed
- synthetic-origin

Evidence with missing provenance may not automatically become institutional truth.

---

# Freshness States

Minimum freshness states:
- current
- aging
- stale
- expired
- superseded

Freshness should consider:
- source age
- environmental change
- dependency volatility
- mission sensitivity
- contradiction emergence

Verification based on stale evidence may require replay.

---

# Confidence Levels

Confidence represents institutional support strength.

Confidence is not truth.

Minimum confidence levels:
- minimal
- weak
- moderate
- strong
- high
- provisional-high

High confidence does not eliminate uncertainty.

Confidence should remain revisable.

---

# Confidence Formation

Confidence formation should consider:
- evidence quality
- provenance quality
- corroboration independence
- contradiction pressure
- reproducibility
- freshness
- transformation distance
- verification depth
- adversarial exposure

Confidence must not derive primarily from:
- repetition
- authority pressure
- popularity
- narrative coherence
- runtime persuasion

---

# Contradiction Weighting

Contradictions are weighted by:
- source integrity
- reproducibility
- scope impact
- dependency impact
- freshness
- verification depth

Minor contradictions may coexist with operational acceptance.

Critical contradictions may trigger:
- restoration
- escalation
- mission suspension
- verification replay
- topology reassessment

---

# Corroboration Semantics

Corroboration requires meaningful independence.

Weak corroboration examples:
- repeated summaries of the same source
- synthetic restatements
- circular citation chains
- mirrored runtime outputs

Strong corroboration favors:
- independent retrieval
- independent methodology
- direct evidence
- separate provenance chains

---

# Transformation Visibility

Evidence transformations must remain reconstructable.

Transformations include:
- summarization
- translation
- synthesis
- extraction
- compression
- interpretation
- aggregation
- modeling

Hidden transformation chains are prohibited.

---

# Dependency Impact

Evidence may affect:
- mission outcomes
- verification conclusions
- topology structure
- authority decisions
- restoration requirements
- institutional memory

Dependency impact should remain visible.

Critical evidence invalidation may require cascading restoration.

---

# Evidence Decay

Evidence integrity may decay over time.

Decay signals include:
- stale provenance
- environmental drift
- contradictory emergence
- dependency invalidation
- unverifiable reproduction
- missing audit continuity

Evidence decay may trigger:
- replay
- revalidation
- restoration
- contradiction escalation

---

# Evidence Drift

Evidence drift occurs when institutions gradually weaken evidence expectations.

Signals include:
- screenshots replacing reproducibility
- confidence replacing traceability
- summaries replacing primary sources
- synthetic outputs treated as direct observation
- unresolved contradictions normalized
- provenance ignored for convenience

Evidence drift is institutional corruption.

# Quantitative Semantics Warning

The Evidence Schema supports structured weighting.

However:

Citadel must avoid collapsing epistemology into simplistic scoring systems.

Numeric scoring alone cannot fully represent:
- uncertainty
- contradiction nuance
- contextual dependency
- adversarial complexity
- institutional judgment

Quantification supports governance.

It does not replace reasoning.

# Authority Interface

Authority may define:
- required evidence depth
- corroboration thresholds
- freshness requirements
- replay conditions

Governance pressure does not strengthen weak evidence.

---

# Runtime Independence

Evidence semantics belong to Citadel doctrine.

They do not belong to:
- a runtime
- a vendor
- a model
- a tooling ecosystem

Runtimes may emit evidence.

They do not define institutional evidence legitimacy.

---

# Noncompliance

An actor violates evidence doctrine if it:
- fabricates provenance
- suppresses contradictory evidence
- launders synthetic outputs into direct evidence
- hides transformation chains
- inflates confidence without support
- weakens corroboration standards silently
- ignores freshness decay
- compresses uncertainty theatrically

Evidence noncompliance requires review and possible restoration.
