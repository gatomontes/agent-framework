# Citadel Consequence Classification

## Purpose

Consequence Classification defines how The Citadel categorizes operational risk and determines the required governance intensity for an operation.

Not all work deserves identical governance pressure.

Classification exists to preserve proportional operational discipline.

---

# Core Principle

Governance intensity should scale with consequence.

Overgovernance creates paralysis.

Undergovernance creates instability.

---

# Consequence Tiers

## TRIVIAL

Minimal operational impact.

Examples:

- formatting adjustments
- cosmetic wording
- disposable experimentation
- low-impact drafts

Characteristics:

- low persistence risk
- low governance sensitivity
- reversible
- low trust impact

Typical requirements:

- lightweight verification
- minimal audit
- optional critique

---

## ROUTINE

Operationally meaningful but low systemic risk.

Examples:

- standard documentation
- non-critical implementation
- ordinary workflow execution
- creative drafting

Characteristics:

- moderate persistence
- recoverable failure
- ordinary operational exposure

Typical requirements:

- verification required
- critique recommended
- audit optional

---

## IMPORTANT

Operationally significant.

Failure meaningfully affects trust, continuity, architecture, or workflow integrity.

Examples:

- architectural decisions
- persistent repo changes
- workflow governance updates
- operational automation
- staffing orchestration

Characteristics:

- meaningful operational persistence
- elevated trust sensitivity
- restoration may be required after failure

Typical requirements:

- verification mandatory
- critique strongly recommended
- escalation sensitivity elevated
- audit conditionally required

---

## CRITICAL

Failure could severely compromise:

- trustworthiness
- governance integrity
- security
- financial correctness
- operational continuity
- institutional coherence

Examples:

- payroll calculations
- security remediation
- authority system changes
- constitutional governance updates
- autonomous execution permissions
- destructive operations

Characteristics:

- high persistence impact
- high trust sensitivity
- difficult restoration
- severe operational consequences

Typical requirements:

- independent verification mandatory
- critique mandatory
- audit strongly required
- escalation aggressively enabled
- restoration rigor elevated
- durable artifact retention mandatory

---

# Classification Inputs

Classification should consider:

- operational persistence
- reversibility
- trust sensitivity
- governance impact
- financial impact
- security impact
- runtime autonomy level
- restoration difficulty
- institutional continuity exposure

---

# Classification Drift

Long-running systems tend to:

- inflate trivial work
- normalize critical risk
- downgrade governance pressure for convenience

Consequence classification must therefore remain reviewable.

---

# Reclassification

Operations may be reclassified when:

- scope expands
- persistence increases
- autonomy increases
- trust sensitivity changes
- governance exposure changes
- new evidence emerges

Reclassification must preserve operational lineage.

---

# Constitutional Rule

A system that treats all work equally eventually governs nothing correctly.
