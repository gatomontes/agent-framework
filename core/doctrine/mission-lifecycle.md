# Mission Lifecycle

## Status

Canonical Citadel doctrine.

This document defines how work exists, progresses, transforms, pauses, restores, escalates, concludes, and persists inside Citadel-governed operations.

A mission is not merely a task.

A mission is a governed operational flow with:
- objectives
- authority
- topology
- verification
- restoration semantics
- audit traceability
- final disposition

---

# Purpose

The Mission Lifecycle exists to prevent:
- chaotic execution
- invisible scope expansion
- endless unfinished work
- ambiguous completion
- governance bypass
- verification omission
- orphaned operational artifacts
- institutional memory loss
- runtime-driven operational drift

The lifecycle standardizes how work moves through the institution.

---

# Core Principle

No mission exists outside lifecycle governance.

If work cannot be placed inside the lifecycle, the work is either:
- underspecified
- unauthorized
- operationally immature
- outside institutional scope

---

# Canonical Lifecycle

```txt
Intent
  -> Rook Intake
    -> Classification
      -> Topology Synthesis
        -> Authority Assignment
          -> Execution
            -> Verification
              -> Critique
                -> Restoration or Escalation
                  -> Final Disposition
                    -> Archival
```

The lifecycle is directional but not strictly linear.

A mission may:
- loop backward
- split into sub-missions
- pause
- escalate
- restore
- terminate early

All transitions must remain observable.

---

# Lifecycle Stages

## 1. Intent

Intent is the initial expression of desired outcome.

Intent may be:
- explicit
- ambiguous
- incomplete
- contradictory
- exploratory
- strategic
- operational

Intent alone is not executable.

Intent must not bypass classification.

Intent must first cross the institutional intake boundary defined by:

```txt
/core/contracts/rook-contract.md
```

before it becomes classifiable mission material.

---

## 2. Classification

Classification determines:
- mission type
- risk level
- required professions
- authority requirements
- verification requirements
- governance sensitivity
- reversibility
- external dependency profile
- escalation expectations

Classification may result in:
- direct execution
- research-desk setup
- strategic decomposition
- rejection
- clarification request
- escalation
- sandboxing

Classification is mandatory.

---

## 3. Topology Synthesis

Topology synthesis transforms classified intent into operational structure.

This may include:
- profession slots
- authority chains
- runtime assignments
- verification routes
- critique routes
- escalation paths
- restoration responsibilities
- mission dependencies
- sub-mission decomposition

Topology synthesis belongs to Citadel doctrine, not runtime preference.

---

## 4. Authority Assignment

Before execution, authority must be assigned.

Authority assignment determines:
- who may act
- who may verify
- who may escalate
- who may halt
- who may modify doctrine
- who may communicate externally
- who owns final disposition

Authority must be auditable.

Authority may not be inferred from runtime capability.

---

## 5. Execution

Execution performs operational work.

Execution may include:
- analysis
- generation
- modification
- orchestration
- tool use
- delegation
- communication
- system interaction
- artifact creation

Execution is not completion.

Execution must continuously preserve:
- evidence
- state visibility
- authority boundaries
- constraint awareness
- audit traceability

If the mission is evidence-seeking or research-bearing, execution should instantiate the protocol defined by:

```txt
/core/protocols/research-desk-protocol.md
```

before broad collection or synthesis release begins.

---

## 6. Verification

Verification evaluates whether mission outputs satisfy defined standards.

Verification must assess:
- correctness
- completeness
- evidence quality
- authority compliance
- constraint preservation
- operational coherence
- unintended consequences

Verification authority should remain separate from production authority whenever practical.

Unverified completion is not institutional completion.

---

## 7. Critique

Critique pressures:
- reasoning
- assumptions
- structure
- evidence
- governance
- coherence
- risk exposure
- drift indicators

Critique exists because technically correct work may still be strategically unsound.

Blackquill and similar systems operate primarily in this layer.

---

## 8. Restoration or Escalation

When coherence degrades, missions must either:
- restore
- escalate
- halt
- terminate

Restoration attempts to recover coherent operation.

Escalation transfers unresolved conflict, uncertainty, or authority limitation upward.

Continuing without restoration or escalation creates operational corruption.

---

## 9. Final Disposition

Every mission must end with explicit disposition.

Allowed canonical dispositions:
- completed-verified
- completed-conditional
- failed
- blocked
- halted
- abandoned
- superseded
- archived-incomplete

No mission may silently disappear.

---

## 10. Archival

Archival preserves institutional memory.

Archive records should preserve:
- intent
- classification
- topology
- authority chain
- artifacts
- evidence
- critique outcomes
- restoration history
- disposition
- unresolved concerns
- lessons learned

Institutional memory is a strategic asset.

---

# Mission States

Minimum mission states:
- proposed
- classified
- assembling
- authorized
- executing
- verifying
- under-critique
- restoring
- escalating
- blocked
- halted
- completed
- archived

State transitions must be observable.

---

# Mission Identity

Every meaningful mission should possess:
- mission identifier
- creation timestamp
- originating authority
- classification metadata
- operational scope
- assigned topology
- current state
- disposition status

Mission identity must survive runtime replacement.

---

# Mission Scope

Every mission must define scope.

Scope includes:
- operational boundaries
- systems affected
- permitted actions
- excluded actions
- authority boundaries
- success conditions
- verification expectations

Scope expansion requires review or escalation.

Silent expansion is prohibited.

---

# Mission Decomposition

Large missions may decompose into sub-missions.

Sub-missions must preserve:
- parent mission reference
- authority inheritance rules
- verification requirements
- escalation routes
- restoration semantics

Sub-missions are not independent unless explicitly detached.

---

# Persistent Missions

Persistent or long-running missions require:
- periodic review
- authority freshness validation
- drift checks
- restoration checkpoints
- archival snapshots
- mission heartbeat visibility

Long-running autonomous operation without review is prohibited.

---

# Mission Drift

Mission drift occurs when operational behavior diverges from authorized intent.

Signals include:
- scope expansion
- objective mutation
- endless refinement
- runtime-driven priorities
- verification degradation
- undocumented dependency growth
- disappearing accountability
- stale assumptions
- institutional memory erosion

Mission drift requires:
- halt
- restoration
- reclassification
- escalation
- or mission termination

---

# Restoration Semantics

Restoration exists to recover coherence.

Restoration may include:
- rollback
- authority reset
- topology redesign
- reassignment
- scope reduction
- verification replay
- contradiction resolution
- artifact reconstruction

Restoration attempts must be documented.

---

# Escalation Semantics

Escalation occurs when:
- authority is insufficient
- uncertainty exceeds tolerance
- contradictions persist
- governance conflict appears
- verification cannot conclude
- restoration fails
- irreversible impact risk emerges
- mission redesign becomes necessary

Escalation must identify:
- blocker
- affected scope
- authority gap
- unresolved uncertainty
- recommended decision point

---

# Lifecycle Integrity Rules

No stage may silently bypass:
- verification
- critique
- restoration
- authority assignment
- final disposition
- archival

Deadline pressure may narrow scope.

It may not silently lower evidence standards.

Fast execution paths may exist, but the lifecycle semantics remain mandatory.

---

# Institutional Rule

Missions belong to the institution, not the runtime.

Runtimes participate in missions.

They do not define the lifecycle itself.

---

# Final Rule

A mission ends only when its disposition is explicit and auditable.

Untracked work becomes institutional entropy.
